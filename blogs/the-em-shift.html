The EM Shift

Making the leap from Individual Contributor (IC) to Engineering Manager (EM) is one of the most challenging and rewarding transitions in a tech career. It's not a promotion — it's a career change. The skills that made you a great engineer are necessary but far from sufficient for leading a team effectively.

The Mindset Shift

As an IC, your value is measured by the code you write, the systems you design, and the problems you solve. As an EM, your value is measured by the output of your team. This requires a fundamental shift: from "how can I fix this?" to "how can I empower the team to build a sustainable solution?"

Early on, I struggled with the urge to jump into code reviews and solve every technical challenge myself. The turning point came when I realized that doing so was actually hurting the team — it created bottlenecks, reduced their ownership, and slowed their growth.

Balancing Technical Depth

One of the biggest fears engineers have about becoming an EM is losing technical depth. The reality is nuanced: you shift from being the deepest expert to being the broadest. You need to understand system design, make architectural trade-off decisions, and evaluate technical proposals — but you don't need to be the one writing the implementation.

I maintain my technical edge by staying close to design reviews, reading production code (not writing it), and carving out time for personal projects and technical writing. This blog is part of that practice.

What Actually Matters

The three things that have the highest leverage as an EM are: hiring well, setting clear context, and removing blockers. Everything else — process improvements, tool choices, sprint ceremonies — is in service of those three pillars.

If you hire great people, give them clear direction, and stay out of their way (while being available when they need you), the team will deliver. The challenge is having the discipline to trust the process and resist the urge to micromanage.

The EM role isn't for everyone, and that's okay. But for those who find joy in multiplying the impact of others, it's incredibly fulfilling.
